CHI 2018 in Review

Mon 30 April 2018 Mon 30 April 2018
By Jacob Thebault-Spieker

There are a number of different ways people write up summaries of conferences they go to. Dan Cosley writes up trip reports, I have seen others write up their favorite papers1, and others just tweet.

CHI is huge, so I think a structured “favorites” list is not actually representative of the conference. It would be impossible for someone attending every session to see everything2, and it is not uncommon for interesting things to be happening simultaneously. Unfortunately, I was unable to attend every session for work reasons, so there were some sessions I needed to skip even though I was excited about some of the talks. I was, however, careful to protect the meal and coffee breaks at the conference, some of the talk sessions I wanted to see, the sessions I presented in, and the session I was chairing.

So in this “Jake’s CHI 2018 in Review” post, I am going to try to emphasize: (1) good questions I heard, (2) the cool talks I saw, (3) the papers that looked cool that I didn’t get to see, (4) a summary of the session I chaired (which I picked because I thought it looked interesting), (5) the sessions I presented in, and (6) fun conversations I had. Hopefully this highly-subjective amalgamation of CHI 2018 will be interesting and helpful to others.

Good Questions I Heard

Dan Cosley, as one part of what I see as his proactive ‘care and maintenance’ of our community likes to say “questions are academic love”. He has raised this point at every conference I’ve ever seen him attend, and always as an exhortation to be thoughtful and considerate about the community we’re a part of. In that spirit, I wanted to note a few of the questions I heard that made me think, and I’m glad were asked.

The first question that I was particularly intrigued by was raised (twice) during the Sharing/New Economy session. Paraphrased, the question was “Our internal data shows that when drivers have more autonomy, they make decisions that are economically worse for them, and they make decisions that lead to increased bias. What do you think we should do?” This tension is particularly interesting to me, given my own work on geographic biases in the sharing economy that I presented later in the session. I noted this question the first time it was raised, and the questioner asked the question again after my talk as well. We think our work speaks to this issue, in part because we our work suggests that the biases are a result of drivers’ “mental maps” (or geographic mental models of the world), and that presents an opportunity to design interventions that don’t need to be as coarse as minimizing driver autonomy about which rides they give or don’t.

A Summary of the Crowd(sourcing) Session

This was the first time I took on the role of chairing a session at CHI, and its a somewhat daunting task. I felt like it was important to make sure I had prepared questions for each of the talks (because ‘questions are academic love’), which meant engaging with each of the papers being presented beforehand.

The first talk in this session was presented by Virginia Tech’s own Kurt Luther on behalf of his former student Divit Singh. In this project, they discussed a system to help experts in cellular biology using crowd workers.3 In particular, there is a complex graph-layout problem in biology related to how cells work. Based on deeply understanding expert process, they showed that the task could be broken down into 4 goals, which crowd workers can perform pretty well at. Crowds perform better than automated graph-layout methods, and in some cases on par with domain experts.

The Sessions I Presented In

I presented in two sessions, but I went a little crazy with the self-promotion while at the conference, so I want to also point to the other cool talks in these sessions.

Sharing/New Economy

Session Chair: Siân Lindley (@SianLindley)

The first talk7 in this session was presented by Ning Ma, a student at Penn State University. Their work focused on understanding Uber drivers and their relationships to Uber the platform through the lens of Stakeholder Theory. The talk itself focused on drivers as stakeholders within an organization, and they suggested increasing driver autonomy as one of the major design implications of their work. This implication led to what I found to be one of the most interesting questions I heard, raised by an Uber employee (as I discussed above).

The second talk8 in this session was presented by Shagun Jhaver, a student at Georgia Tech, reporting on work he did as an intern at Airbnb. This work focused on what they called “double negotiation” that hosts experience: needing to attend to attracting guests to stay with them, and their perceptions of the expectations imposed by the algorithms they are subject to. Their findings suggest that Airbnb hosts experience algorithmic anxiety, and one of the ways they found that hosts deal with this anxiety is to try to reverse engineer the platform’s algorithms.

The third talk9 in this session was presented by Ming Yin, a postdoc with Microsoft Research in the Computational Social Science group. This work sought to understand the economic value that workers in Mechanical Turk place on increasing the flexibility (operationalized as amount of time allotted) they are given about when to complete the HIT they accept. I thought this was really interesting work, and I was really curious about how Ming thinks about the tension between giving workers flexibility, and the kinds of work on Turk that necessitate speedy participation (e.g. to power a live system). The economic metric is an interesting one, and suggests the need to pay a premium for workers who will respond quickly. A clear next step is understanding if paying the premium actually does adjust behavior, or whether the economic metric is a side-effect of some other underlying motivational process.

The fourth talk in this session was presented by me, about a TOCHI article10 from when I was a student.
